Color Theory — Working Reference

One wheel. Every rule.

Drag the wheel to set a base hue. Everything below — tints, shades, harmonies, and a full website palette (buttons, hover, shadows, warnings, notifications) — is generated automatically from that one color.

01

Pick a base hue

Hue is the pure color itself — where it sits on the wheel, measured in degrees (0–360°). Saturation is its intensity (gray → vivid). Lightness is how close it sits to black or white. Every color you'll ever mix is just these three dials.

02

Tints, shades & tones

Three ways to extend one color into a usable range, instead of reaching for a new hue every time you need something lighter or more muted.

Tints — mix with white → lighter, airier. Good for backgrounds, hover states.
Shades — mix with black → deeper, heavier. Good for text, dark-mode surfaces.
Tones — mix with gray → muted, quieter. Good for large surfaces that shouldn't shout.
03

Color harmonies

Where you place supporting colors on the wheel relative to your base — this is what most "how do I pick colors that go together" questions are really asking.

Complementary

Base + its opposite (180° away). Maximum contrast, high energy. Use one as the dominant color and the other only as a small accent — never 50/50.

Analogous

Base + neighbors (±30°). Naturally harmonious, low tension — how gradients and calm palettes are built. Pick one to dominate.

Split-complementary

Base + the two colors beside its complement (150°/210°). Same visual punch as complementary, gentler to look at.

Triadic

Three colors evenly spaced (120° apart). Vibrant and balanced; let one lead and use the other two as accents, not equals.

Tetradic (square)

Four colors, 90° apart. Rich but risky — needs one clear dominant color and one dominant temperature or it turns to noise.

Monochromatic

One hue, varied lightness only. Zero risk, guaranteed cohesion — the safest professional default when in doubt.

04

Supporting colors — the 60/30/10 rule

Harmony tells you which colors go together. Ratio tells you how much of each to actually use. Pick one dominant, one secondary, one accent — and keep them unequal.

60% Dominant — background, primary surface. Usually your base color as a tint or a neutral.
30% Secondary — supporting sections, cards, secondary UI. An analogous or muted tone.
10% Accent — buttons, links, alerts. Your highest-contrast color, used sparingly on purpose.

Quick cheat sheet

  

    
Hue
The pure color, 0–360° on the wheel (red → yellow → green → blue → red).

    
Saturation
Intensity of the hue — 0% is gray, 100% is fully vivid.

    
Lightness
Position between black (0%) and white (100%).

    
Tint
Hue + white. Lighter, softer, airier.

    
Shade
Hue + black. Deeper, heavier, more serious.

    
Tone
Hue + gray. Muted, sophisticated, less saturated.

    
Complementary
Opposite hues (180°). Max contrast — use unevenly.

    
Analogous
Neighboring hues (±30°). Calm, natural cohesion.

    
Triadic
Three hues 120° apart. Balanced and vibrant.

    
Split-complementary
Base + both neighbors of its complement. Contrast, less tension.

    
Monochromatic
One hue, many lightness/saturation steps. Safest default.

    
60/30/10
Dominant / secondary / accent ratio for any palette.

    
Background vs. Surface
Background is the page canvas (near-white/neutral tint). Surface is what sits on it — cards, panels — usually a touch lighter for separation.

    
Hover / Active states
Same hue, darker shade. ~12% darker for hover, ~22% for active/pressed — never a different color.

    
Shadows
Never pure black. A low-opacity, hue-tinted dark shade of your base reads as "elevation," not a gray smudge.

    
Status colors
Success (green), Warning (amber), Error (red), Info (blue) stay on recognizable hues by convention — but tune their saturation to match your base so they feel native to the palette, not bolted on.

    
Gradients
Stay in-family: blend tints/shades of one hue for calm gradients, or base + analogous for a livelier one. Avoid gradienting across a full complementary jump — it muddies in the middle.

    
Dark mode
Don't just invert. Backgrounds get dark, near-desaturated; text goes light; and interactive hover/active states lighten instead of darken, since darkening is invisible on a dark canvas.
